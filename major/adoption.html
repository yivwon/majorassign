Adoption

From the tiny Chihuahua to the towering Great Dane, dog breeds come in all shapes, sizes, and personalities! No matter the breed, every pup brings something special to the pack!

Checkpoint... Are you ready for a dog?

  • Do I have time to give my dog lots of love and attention?
  • Can take my dog on a walk three times a day?
  • Do I have a dog allergy?!
  • Do I travel often- and have plan for pet care when I do?
  • Am I ready for vet bills, vaccines, and emergencies?
  • Can I afford food, grooming, toys, and regular supplies?
  • Will I need pet insurance or emergency savings for my dog?
  • Can I be patient during training (accidents, barking, chewing)?
  • Will I provide enough mental stimulation?
  • Do I understand that dogs aren't just pets‚Äîthey're family?

Matching your energy

High-energy breeds need lots of exercise and mental stimulation, while some are more low-key. Some high energy dogs are Border Collies, Labrador Retrievers, and Jask Russell Terriers. Examples of low-energy dogs are Basset Hounds, Bulldogs, and Cavalier King Charles Spaniel.

Size of dog

Consider your living space. A Saint Bernard in a studio apartment? Maybe not the best match. Some examples of big dogs are Great Danes, German Shepherds, and a Bernese Mountain Dog. Examples of a small dog are Chihuahuas, Pomeranians, and French Bulldogs.

Age considerations

Puppies need lots of training. Adult or senior dogs may already be house-trained and calmer.

üè• First Few Days at Home

  • Give your dog time to adjust‚Äînew smells, sights, and sounds can be overwhelming.
  • Set a routine for meals, potty breaks, and walks right away.
  • Introduce your dog slowly to different rooms, family members, and other pets.
  • Create a cozy, quiet spot where your dog can retreat and feel safe.
  • Avoid overwhelming them with too many new people or outings in the first few days.
  • Reward calm behavior with treats and gentle affection.
  • Watch for signs of stress (panting, hiding, pacing) and give them space when needed.
  • Be patient‚Äîthis is a big transition for them, and every dog adjusts at their own pace!

üß† Training

Start training early‚Äîeven basic commands like sit, stay, and come go a long way. Try to use positive reinforcement: treats, praise, and patience make learning fun. Keep training sessions short and consistent to avoid overwhelming your dog. Avoid punishment‚Äîredirect unwanted behavior instead and celebrate progress!

üêæ Making Friends (Socialization)

Gently introduce your dog to new people, pets, and environments at their pace. Take short walks in different areas to help them get used to new sights and smells. Reward calm behavior when meeting others to build positive associations. Sign up for a puppy class or group walks if they're comfortable‚Äîit's great practice!

üêæ Vet Care & Wellness

  • Book a vet appointment within the first week.
  • Stay on top of vaccinations, flea/tick prevention, and annual checkups.
  • Ask your vet about spaying/neutering if not already done.
  • Keep a folder (digital or paper) with all vet records and vaccination history.
  • Watch for changes in appetite, behavior, or energy‚Äîthese can be early signs something's off.
